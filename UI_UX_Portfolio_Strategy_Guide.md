## The 2026 Landscape: What's Changed

The product design hiring market in 2026 has shifted significantly. Hiring managers now spend an average of **6-8 seconds** on an initial portfolio scan before deciding whether to dig deeper. They then spend roughly **3-5 minutes** skimming if you pass that first filter. Two major shifts define this year:

1. **Business impact over aesthetics** — Every case study should open with "What was the business problem?" not "Here are my wireframes." Managers want to see metrics: conversion lifts, drop-off reductions, time-on-task improvements.

2. **AI proficiency is now a hiring filter** — 78% of design managers now consider AI tool proficiency when evaluating candidates. They want to see how you use AI as a creative partner without losing your own judgment.

Your positioning as a fintech designer who works across platforms (web, tablet, mobile) and spans from design systems to detailed screens is already strong. The key is how you frame and present it.

## How to Select Projects From Your Large Body of Work

You mentioned having "too much to show." Here's a systematic framework to narrow it down:

### Step 1: The Matrix Method

Create a simple matrix with your projects on one axis and these criteria on the other. Score each 1-5:

| Criteria | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Business Impact** | Can you cite metrics, revenue impact, or user growth? |
| **Design Complexity** | Does it show deep thinking, not just execution? |
| **Role Clarity** | Was your contribution substantial and clearly attributable? |
| **Visual Polish** | Does the final output look impressive in screenshots? |
| **Story Potential** | Is there a compelling before/after or problem-to-solution arc? |
| **Relevance to Target Role** | Does it match what fintech/product design roles are hiring for? |

Score your projects. Pick the top 4.

### Step 2: Ensure Variety Across Your 4 Projects

Your 4 case studies should collectively demonstrate:

**Platform coverage** — At least one should prominently feature responsive/multi-platform work (web + tablet + mobile). This is your differentiator.

**Scope coverage** — Show different scales of work:
- One project showing end-to-end product design (0 to 1 or major redesign)
- One project showing design system work (tokens, components, documentation)
- One project showing a focused feature or flow optimization (e.g., onboarding, payments, dashboards)
- One project showing cross-platform consistency and adaptation

**Skill coverage** — Across all 4, reviewers should see evidence of: user research, information architecture, interaction design, visual design, prototyping, design systems thinking, and data-informed iteration.

### Step 3: The "Fintech Filter"

Since you're targeting fintech roles, prioritize projects that demonstrate:

- Complex data visualization and financial dashboards
- Trust-building design patterns (security indicators, transaction confirmations)
- Simplifying complex financial workflows for regular users
- Compliance-aware design (KYC flows, regulatory requirements)
- Personalization and AI-driven experiences
- Multi-platform consistency for financial tools

## Recommended Portfolio Structure

### Homepage / Landing

- Your name, title, and a one-line positioning statement
- Suggested positioning: **"Product Designer specializing in fintech experiences across web, tablet, and mobile — from design systems architecture to pixel-perfect screens"**
- 4 project cards with strong hero images, project title, brief descriptor, and your role
- Put your strongest/most recent project first

### Each Case Study (800-1,500 words + visuals)

Follow this structure for maximum impact:

**1. The Hook (2-3 sentences)**
Open with the business problem and a compelling metric. Example: "Our payment flow had a 42% drop-off rate, costing the business an estimated $X in failed transactions monthly."

**2. Context & Role**
Company, timeline, team size, your specific role. Be explicit: "I was the sole product designer responsible for..."

**3. Research & Discovery**
What you learned from users. Include artifacts: interview quotes, journey maps, competitive analysis snippets. Show the messy middle — hiring managers want to see how you think, not just what you shipped.

**4. Design Exploration**
Wireframes, sketches, early concepts. Show the options you considered and why you chose the direction you did. Include dead ends — they demonstrate judgment.

**5. Solution**
High-fidelity screens, interaction details, micro-interactions. This is where you showcase visual craft. Show multi-platform variations if applicable.

**6. Design System Connection**
How your work connected to or evolved the design system. This is unique to your profile — use it. Show component decisions, token usage, documentation.

**7. Results & Impact**
Metrics, user feedback, business outcomes. If you don't have exact numbers, use qualitative results: "Reduced support tickets related to payments by approximately 60%."

**8. Reflection**
What you'd do differently. This shows self-awareness and growth mindset.

### About Page

- Brief bio emphasizing fintech expertise
- Your design philosophy (keep it to 2-3 sentences)
- Tools and methods you use (including AI tools — this is a 2026 differentiator)
- Link to resume/LinkedIn

## How to Position Your AI Skills (The 2026 Differentiator)

This is where you stand out. Showing that you use Claude effectively as a design partner is not a gimmick — it's what 78% of hiring managers now look for. Here's how to weave it in:

### What to Show

- **AI-assisted research synthesis** — How you use Claude to process user research data, identify patterns across interviews, or generate initial affinity diagrams
- **Design exploration acceleration** — Using AI to rapidly explore copy variations, generate component documentation, or stress-test edge cases
- **Design system documentation** — Claude skills for generating component specs, usage guidelines, or token documentation
- **Workflow integration** — How AI fits into your end-to-end process without replacing your judgment

### What NOT to Do

- Don't make AI the centerpiece — it's a tool in your process, not a replacement for your thinking
- Don't claim AI "designed" anything — you design, AI accelerates
- Don't list AI tools like they're skills — show them in context of real project outcomes

### Suggested Portfolio Section

Add a brief section (on your About page or as a callout in case studies) like:

> **"I integrate AI tools — particularly Claude — into my design workflow for research synthesis, rapid prototyping, design system documentation, and edge-case testing. This lets me move faster from insight to execution while maintaining design judgment and craft."**

## Fintech-Specific Portfolio Tips

### What Fintech Hiring Managers Value Most

1. **Simplification of complexity** — Can you take a complex financial workflow and make it intuitive? This is the #1 skill they look for.

2. **Trust through design** — Trust in fintech is a design decision, not a legal disclaimer. Show how your design choices (visual hierarchy, confirmation patterns, transparency) build user confidence.

3. **Data-rich interfaces** — Financial products are inherently data-heavy. Show that you can design meaningful data visualizations and dashboards that help users make decisions.

4. **Multi-platform competency** — Fintech users switch between web and mobile constantly. Your cross-platform experience is a major asset — showcase it prominently.

5. **Compliance awareness** — Show that you understand the constraints of designing in a regulated industry. KYC flows, privacy considerations, and accessibility aren't obstacles — they're design challenges you've solved.

### Fintech Case Study Angles That Stand Out

- Before/after of a complex financial flow you simplified
- How you designed a dashboard that surfaces the right data at the right moment
- A multi-platform experience where you maintained consistency while adapting to each context
- A design system component that scales across products and platforms
- An AI-personalized experience (recommendations, insights, nudges)

## Portfolio Platform & Technical Tips

**Recommended platforms:** A custom site (Framer, Webflow, or code) signals craft. Notion or Behance are acceptable but don't differentiate. Avoid PDF-only portfolios.

**Mobile responsiveness:** Your portfolio itself must be flawless on mobile. You're a multi-platform designer — if your own portfolio breaks on mobile, that's an instant credibility hit.

**Load speed:** Keep it fast. Compress images. Hiring managers abandon slow portfolios.

**Password protection:** Fine for NDA work, but have at least 1-2 public projects. Locked-down portfolios get skipped.

**Case study length:** Target 800-1,500 words per case study. Add a "TL;DR" or summary card at the top of each for the 6-second scanners.
